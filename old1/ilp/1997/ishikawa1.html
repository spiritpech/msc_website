PIVOT FRICTION COMPENSATION USING ACCELEROMETER AND DISTURBANCE OBSERVER FOR HARD DISK DRIVES

Jun Ishikawa

PROJECT DESCRIPTION

The embedded sector servo system has become common for hard disk drives (HDDs) with higher track densities. In this servo system, servo information is embedded in circular data tracks at equally spaced angles, i.e., sectors, so that the magnetic head may be controlled directly relative to the target track. The number of serctors should be minimized for securing the maximum data storage area, but this will lower the sampling frequency for digital control based on position error feedback. Thus, it is a challenge to design a controller which provides a necessary positioning accuracy to match the track density at low frequency sampling of the error. While disk manufacturers have successfully utilized modern approaches such as model following control and multi-rate control to achieve the goal, an obstacle for further enhancement of performance is represented by nonlinearities and uncertainties in the drive unit such as pivot friction. This research will present a friction compensator using the combination of accelerometer and disturbance observer.

METHODOLOGY

  • Estimate the pivot friction torque using the scaled voice coil motor (VCM) current and the scaled carriage arm acceleration (Disturbance Observer).
  • Linearize the dynamics between the VCM current, c, and the carriage arm acceleration, a, to be a constant gain within an observer bandwidth by feeding back the filter output, which is the friction estimate, into a VCM amplifier (Friction Compensator).
  • Design the low pass filter in Fig.JI-1 using the stocahstic estimation theory (Kalman Filter). See Fig.JI-2 for the whole configuration of the friction compensator.
  • Implement the proposed observer as an analog system to enlarge the observer bandwidth and reduce the load on a lower sampling rate positioning loop.

RESULTS TO DATE

  • Showed by simulatioin that the mechanical nonlinearity resulting form pivot friction can be eliminated by the proposed method.
    • The mechanical system under the influence of friction exhibits varying low frequency gain depending on the input magnitude, or more precisely depending on the carriage arm displacement (Fig.JI-3 a). This kind of perturbation causes not only the gain reduction at frequencies, but also critical modeling errors for model-based control.
    • The pivot friction compensator linearized the characteristics of the mechanical system as a double integrator, i.e., as a pure inertia system, no matter what the input magnitude was (Fig.JI-3 b).
  • Investigate the practicality of the proposed approach by experimentation.
