Fuzzy Systems

Since the first reported successful application of fuzzy systems theory to feedback control in the mid 1970s, applications of fuzzy control have steadily increased. Although the approach is often referred to as fuzzy control, it has two components: "rule or logic base" and "fuzzy set theory." Experts' knowledge of good strategic ideas can often be described by a set of linguistic rules. The development of rules for a controlled plant is supported by the designer's good understanding of how the system works. Fuzzy set theory provides a means to transform a set of rules to a computer implementable control algorithm. The most notable applications are the wide variety of commercial products in Japan which use fuzzy rule base control and technology. Europe and the United States are also showing increased interest inthe technology in industrial automation as well as commercial products.

In contrast to traditional approaches for the design and analysis of control systems, which begin with mathematical models that describe the dynamics of the physical system to be controlled, the fuzzy rule base design approach was introduced to bypass explicit mathematical modeling and analysis. Two things should be noted here: one is that the designer must have a model (implicit model) although it may not be in a differential equation form, and the other is that the fuzzy linquistic rule base is a friendly interface to human experts in order to describe and convert his/her knowledge to control algorithms. In fact, good "engineering" or "ideas" appears to be the basis behind successful implementations of fuzzy control. The use of a fuzzy rule base approach is not limited to a signal regulation level, which is often only one aspect of control/automation systems. Fuzzy logic is an attractive method for higher level control such as coordination fo various sub-controllers, each of which is designed for a different objective. A simple but instructive example for this is the swinging-up and stabilization of an inverted pendulum. Fuzzy counterparts of various control methodologies, such as predictive control and adaptive control, have been reported. While the fuzzy counterparts do not necessarily use the same, or even similar, algorithm as the conventional methodologies, the motivation and goal are common. It is expected that increased attention will be directed to the blending of conventional control and fuzzy control. In fact, the design of the fuzzy control algorithm can often be developed based on the experience and knowledge of experts such as plant operators, engineers, and plant designers.
