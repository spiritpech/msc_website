Preface

Mechanical systems are becoming increasingly reliant on sophisticated methods of sensing, signal processing, and control to improve performance. Machine tools, vehicles, data storage devices, and positioning systems are but a few examples. At the same time, rapid developments in microelectronics and computing have made it possible to use a broad spectrum of signal processing and control methods in real-time in a very cost-effective fashion. It is likely that many future advances in mechanical systems will depend upon further application of sophisticated control systems.

My research group addresses a broad range of topics in the general area of control systems. These include applied mathematical analysis, computer simulation, and hardware/software implementation. The title Control of Mechanical Systems reflects the fact that mechanical systems are the fundamental devices at the center of each project. Theoretical research is pursued in control methodologies such as adaptive and learning control, optimal control, robust control, fuzzy control, nonlinear control, and system identification. Application oriented research concerns robotics, machining centers, precision X-Y positioning systems, intelligent vehicle highway systems, and data storage devices. It is a Berkeley tradition to blend theory and application and this tradition can be found in almost all of the projects in my group.

The department offers a variety of courses at the graduate level to help prepare students for advanced research and development work. Examples include courses in linear sustems theory, digital control, modeling and identification, nonlinear control, adaptive control, real-time software, application of microcontrollers for signal processing and control, manufacturing system processes, and robotics. I encourage my students to broaden their backgrounds by taking relevant courses in other departments such as Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, as well as Mathematics.

This document provides a brief outline of research projects conducted during the 1996-1997 academic year. This work has been sponsored by a number of sources in government and industry. In prticular, I wish to express my gratitude to the industrial sponsors who have generously supported our research. Furthermore, they have offered valuable insight and suggestions that habe helped focus our efforts in critical areas. I believe that this interaction between the university and industry is important in maintaining a strong research program which addresses fundamental issues and yet remains relevant to current engineering challenges Finally, I welcome quistions and comments, Thank you.
